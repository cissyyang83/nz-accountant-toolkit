# Tax Concepts Used in This Toolkit

This page summarises common New Zealand accounting and tax concepts referenced in the toolkit.

It is a plain-English orientation document only. Practitioners should confirm current Inland Revenue guidance and legislation before relying on any tax position.

## GST

Goods and Services Tax is commonly reviewed by comparing GST returns, Xero GST reports, GST control accounts, Inland Revenue filing history, and balance sheet movements.

Typical review matters include:

- Output tax on sales.
- Input tax on purchases.
- Zero-rated and exempt supplies.
- Private use adjustments.
- Mixed-use assets.
- Entertainment adjustments.
- Bad debt adjustments.
- GST on asset purchases and disposals.

## FIF

Foreign Investment Fund rules may apply when a New Zealand tax resident holds certain overseas investments.

Common methods include:

- FDR: fair dividend rate, commonly calculated as 5 percent of opening market value where applicable.
- CV: comparative value, based on the movement in value plus gains and income during the year.

The toolkit includes a calculator specification that helps compare FDR and CV outcomes, while still requiring professional review of eligibility, exemptions, and method selection.

## FDR

FDR stands for fair dividend rate. It is generally based on a percentage of the opening market value of relevant FIF interests. The specification uses 5 percent as the standard design assumption, subject to current rules and exemptions.

## CV

CV stands for comparative value. It considers the change in market value during the year, plus income and sale proceeds, less purchase costs and opening value. The exact calculation should be reviewed against current rules and client facts.

## Quick Sale Adjustment

This toolkit uses "Quick Sale Adjustment" as a practical workpaper label for reviewing potentially taxable land disposals. The review should consider bright-line, land dealer, developer, builder, subdivision, associated person, intention, and residential exclusion matters.

## Property Development

Property development workpapers often need to track land costs, consents, professional fees, construction, finance, holding costs, GST treatment, sales proceeds, and allocation between taxable and capital components.

## Shareholder Loans

Shareholder current accounts and shareholder loans can create accounting and tax issues where drawings exceed credits. Review may include salaries, dividends, interest, repayments, imputation credits, and overdrawn balances.

## Trust Accounting

Trust accounting requires clear separation between trustee decisions, beneficiary distributions, beneficiary current accounts, trustee expenses, capital transactions, income allocations, and supporting minutes or resolutions.
